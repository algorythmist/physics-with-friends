A Tour of Mathematical Optimization

Optimization is everywhere. When your GPS finds the fastest route, when a hedge fund rebalances a portfolio, when a neural network learns from data — all of these reduce to the same abstract question: how do you find the best solution out of all possible ones?

This article walks through that question systematically, starting from the simplest possible case (one variable, no constraints) and building up to mixed-integer programs that power modern logistics and finance. The math isn't hidden, but the goal is always intuition first.

1. What Does "Optimization" Actually Mean?

At its core, every optimization problem is about minimizing an objective function over some set of allowed inputs , called the feasible set:

That's it. Everything else — gradients, constraints, integer variables — is machinery built on top of this one idea.

A quick note on maximization: it's not a separate problem. Maximizing is identical to minimizing , so without loss of generality we always talk about minimization.

Now, "the best solution" turns out to be a slippery concept. There are two distinct things it could mean:

  • A global minimizer satisfies for every . This is the true best.
  • A local minimizer satisfies only for near — it's the bottom of some local valley, but there might be a deeper valley elsewhere.

Global minima are what we want. Local minima are usually all we can find. The gap between these two is arguably the central tension in all of optimization.

The single most important dividing line is convex vs. nonconvex. In a convex problem, the landscape has only one valley — any local minimum is automatically global, and efficient algorithms are guaranteed to find it. In a nonconvex problem, you might be standing at the bottom of a valley without knowing there's a deeper one two miles over.

Most of deep learning lives in the nonconvex world, which is one reason training large models is so finicky.

6. Linear Programming

A linear program (LP) is the special case where both the objective and all constraints are linear:

This is called standard form. Any LP with inequality constraints can be converted to it by introducing slack variables (replace with , ).

Why LPs Are Special

The feasible set is a convex polyhedron — the intersection of finitely many half-spaces. Since the objective is linear (flat), it has no curvature, which means the minimum is always achieved at a vertex of the polyhedron. This geometric observation is the entire foundation of the simplex method.

The Simplex Method

Proposed by George Dantzig in 1947, simplex exploits the vertex structure directly:

  1. Start at a feasible vertex.
  2. Check whether any neighboring vertex improves the objective using reduced costs.
  3. Pivot to the best improving neighbor.
  4. Repeat until no improving neighbor exists — optimality is certified.

In the worst case, simplex can visit exponentially many vertices. In practice, it's astonishingly fast. Most real LPs are solved in a number of pivots roughly linear in the problem size.

Interior-Point Methods

An alternative approach: rather than hopping between vertices, interior-point methods stay strictly inside the polyhedron and follow a smooth central path to the optimum. They run in provably polynomial time () and are preferred for very large LPs where simplex struggles.

7. Discrete Optimization: Why Integers Make Life Hard

So far everything has been continuous — variables can take any real value and calculus does the work. What happens when the variables are forced to be integers, like or ?

Things break badly, for three fundamental reasons.

No gradient. An integer domain isn't connected. There's no notion of "move slightly in direction ," so gradients and smooth calculus simply don't apply.

Rounding fails. You might think: solve the continuous relaxation, then round to the nearest integer. In general this doesn't work. The rounded solution may be infeasible, and even if it's feasible it can be arbitrarily far from optimal.

No local = global. The feasible set is a discrete lattice — not convex — so local search has no guarantee of finding anything good.

And there's a more brutal problem lurking underneath: combinatorial explosion. For binary variables there are candidate solutions. At , that's more than the number of atoms in the observable universe. Exhaustive search is completely hopeless.

NP-Hardness

Most interesting discrete optimization problems are NP-hard, meaning no polynomial-time algorithm is known and finding one would prove P = NP. The canonical examples:

NP-hardness doesn't mean "impossible" — it means you have to make a tradeoff: either accept algorithms that are slow in the worst case, or accept solutions that might not be optimal.

8. Exact Algorithms for Discrete Optimization

Despite NP-hardness, several algorithms do well in practice by avoiding exhaustive search through intelligent pruning.

Exhaustive Search

The baseline: enumerate everything and keep the best. Correct by definition, practical only for tiny instances ().

Branch and Bound

The standard framework for exact discrete optimization. The key idea is to use the LP relaxation (drop the integrality constraints and solve the continuous version) to get a lower bound on the true optimum, then prune any branch that can't beat the best integer solution found so far.

  1. Relax integrality and solve the LP relaxation → get lower bound .
  2. Branch on a fractional variable, say : spawn two subproblems with and .
  3. Bound each subproblem with its own LP relaxation. If a subproblem's lower bound exceeds the best integer solution found so far (the incumbent), prune it.
  4. Recurse until all subproblems are solved or pruned.

The tighter the LP bound, the more aggressively you can prune, and the faster the algorithm.

Branch and Cut

An enhancement of branch and bound that adds cutting planes at each node: valid inequalities that slice off the current fractional LP solution without removing any integer-feasible point. The effect is to tighten the LP bound, enabling more pruning and less branching. Branch and cut is the core engine of every modern MIP solver (Gurobi, CPLEX, HiGHS).

How Solvers Tackle It

Modern MIP solvers run branch and cut on the LP relaxation — the same approach as Section 8, but now heavily engineered. At each node, the solver:

  • Preprocesses the formulation to tighten bounds before the main solve
  • Adds cutting planes (Gomory cuts, knapsack covers, and dozens of others) to sharpen the LP bound
  • Runs primal heuristics to quickly find good integer solutions that raise the pruning bar
  • Chooses branching variables strategically based on pseudocost estimates

The result is remarkable: practical MIPs with millions of variables are solved routinely in industry, despite worst-case exponential complexity. The gap between theory (NP-hard) and practice (often fast) is one of the more striking facts in applied mathematics.

Wrapping Up

We started from a single equation — — and followed it through increasingly complex terrain: from 1D calculus to gradient descent, from Lagrange multipliers to LP, from NP-hardness to branch and cut.

A few threads worth carrying forward:

  • Convexity is the dividing line. If your problem is convex, life is good: local = global, and efficient algorithms exist. If not, you're navigating a landscape full of traps.
  • The derivative condition is necessary but not sufficient. Always check second-order conditions — or accept that you might be at a saddle rather than a minimum.
  • Integers are fundamentally hard. The jump from continuous to discrete variables changes the problem class from "tractable" to "NP-hard," and no general-purpose trick bridges that gap. What we have instead are algorithms that work well in practice even when theory offers no guarantees.

The field goes much deeper — duality theory, stochastic optimization, optimal control, infinite-dimensional calculus of variations — but these ten sections cover the ground that shows up most often in practice.
